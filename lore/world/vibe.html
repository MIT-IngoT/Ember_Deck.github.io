What this world feels like

Ember Deck is a dieselpunk low‑fantasy setting: think late‑1920s/1930s industry, artillery, engines, and trench maps, but soaked in ash, bad weather, and magical fallout. Magic is everywhere, yet almost nobody uses it well; most people can maybe spark a torch or vent a small surge, while precise, repeatable spellwork is rare and risky. The default tone is dark and gritty: the world is miserable to live in, people assume the worst, and survival choices often matter more than ideals.
The Great War never truly stopped; it shattered into endless local wars over resources and defensible ground. Borders, regimes, and even school maps keep changing every few years as frontlines creep back and forth, so people trust their city, their unit, or their convoy far more than any abstract “nation.” Travel beyond a well‑lit Hearth is frightening even for trained Embers because the GREY MIST, Hollows, and roaming armies make the world outside feel half‑lost and actively hostile.

How magic actually works

In‑world, people talk about “magic,” but underneath it behaves more like fallout from an overtaxed soul‑furnace than like a clean spell system. The first world‑scale war came early, killed staggering numbers of people, and overloaded Hell, which in this setting is a processing furnace for souls rather than a moral destination. When the furnace could not keep up, the overflow bled back into the world as raw, dangerous soul‑energy; that leak is what people now experience as magic.
Because of that origin, magic behaves more like radiation or pressure than like a tame tool. It builds up in living bodies, causing telltale signs like glowing veins and eyes, fever, and cracked skin, and if it is not safely vented or shared through a Hearth, it can trigger collapse into a Hollow. Anyone can coax a small effect into existence, but pushing for more power without training, support, and cooling is like stepping into a reactor core: the strength is there, but precision and survival are not.

The GREY MIST and Hollows

The GREY MIST is the other half of the disaster: it is the cold, impersonal “will” of all that unprocessed soul‑stuff leaking into reality. It does not carry feelings, only fragments of memory and belief, and it clings to low‑light, low‑heat areas; that is why cities burn lanterns even at noon and why darkness is treated as a safety hazard, not an aesthetic choice. Walking into thick Mist can mean hallucinations, personality bleed, and temporary loss of self as your mind is pressed against countless other impressions.
When a person’s internal load of soul‑energy spikes too far, they do not explode outward; instead, their “soul container” implodes and fails. That process strips away memory and identity into the Mist and leaves behind a Hollow: a thermally vitrified, zombie‑like shell that shambles toward heat and life because residual soul‑heat seeks equilibrium. Hollows are exterminated on sight not because they are evil, but because everyone knows they were people yesterday and that collapse can happen to anyone whose system fails.

Hearths, Embers, and Cinders

Player characters are Embers, people whose spark flared bright enough that they can work significant magic but at the cost of burning through their own lifespan. To survive, most Embers are bound into Hearths: tightly linked groups whose soul‑bond spreads the heat and pressure across multiple people, allowing safer venting, shared emotional regulation, and somewhat cleaner channelling of power. The bond shapes both personality and combat style, and mechanically it affects shared energy, damage mitigation, and how well the group fights together.

When a Hearth bond forms, it may be due to ritual, traumatic shared experience, or long, intense closeness; over time, members often lose or blur their memories of who they were before, which weakens traditional ideas of a fixed “self.” If a bond breaks catastrophically and an Ember survives, they become a Cinder: a lone, unstable caster with power but no damping, prone to predation on others or complete mental break. Cinders make excellent antagonists because they know they are burning out and often respond by consuming captives as fuel or lashing out at the world that failed them.

Society, war, and expectations for play

States, cities, and factions treat Hearths, Embers, and even Cinders as weapons first and everything else second. Military service is universal but intentionally short; strategists learned that extended deployment makes people Hollow out faster, so they rotate bodies through the grinder to extract usefulness before collapse. With the Great War fragmented into local conflicts, each resource‑rich or defensible location becomes a prize, and everyone expects conscription, propaganda, and some level of normalized atrocity as part of everyday life.
For players, this means the default expectation is not clean heroism. Characters are allowed—often encouraged—to be compromised: noble in some ways and selfish in others, more concerned with keeping their Hearth alive, securing a working rail line, or preventing a Cinder from devouring a village than with “saving the world.” The setting supports investigative missions, deniable operations, front‑line actions, and dirty survival work where magic feels potent but dangerous, the world pushes back hard, and every victory leaves scars.
